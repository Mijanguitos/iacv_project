\section{Methodology}

\subsection{Lane Detection}
Lane Detection

Lane detection constitutes a fundamental component of the proposed bowling analysis pipeline. Unlike traditional autonomous driving applications, where lane detection is primarily used for navigation, in this work it serves two main purposes: restricting the search region for bowling ball detection and estimating the lane geometry required for perspective rectification. By estimating the lane boundaries, a planar homography can be computed to rectify the image plane into a top-down representation of the bowling lane. This rectification, combined with the assumption that the bowling ball remains in contact with the lane surface and preserves a constant radius, enables the estimation of the ball trajectory in three-dimensional space.
Bowling lane detection presents several challenges, including varying illumination conditions, strong perspective effects, the presence of multiple adjacent lanes with similar geometric structures, and partial occlusions caused by bowling pins. Furthermore, not all lane boundaries exhibit the same visual characteristics. While some borders appear as strong visible edges, others are partially occluded or difficult to identify directly. For this reason, a boundary-specific detection strategy was adopted, where each lane border is estimated using a tailored approach while maintaining a shared preprocessing pipeline.
The proposed lane detection framework consists of three main stages: image preprocessing, edge extraction, and geometric line estimation. Standard preprocessing operations, including color space transformation and Gaussian smoothing, are first applied to enhance relevant structural information and suppress noise. Subsequently, edge detection and Hough-based line extraction are employed, with specialized heuristics introduced depending on the boundary being estimated.




Single-channel image transformation
Since edge detection methods operate on single-channel intensity images, an initial transformation from the RGB image space \mathbb{R}^{n\times m\times3} to a single-channel representation \mathbb{R}^{n\times m}is required. The effectiveness of this transformation directly affects the separability of lane boundaries from the surrounding scene, influencing both edge detection robustness and the sensitivity of subsequent line extraction methods.
The objective of this stage is to maximize the contrast between relevant lane structures and the background while minimizing the effect of illumination variability across videos. Bowling recordings may differ considerably in lighting conditions, reflections on the lane surface, and camera exposure settings, making the choice of transformation particularly important.
As a baseline, the standard grayscale conversion was evaluated conversion [Cita opencv, cita libro]. This transformation computes a weighted combination of the RGB channels according to perceptual luminance:
Y=0.299R+0.587G+0.114B



where R, G, and Brepresent the red, green, and blue image channels, respectively. Although widely used in computer vision applications, this conversion does not explicitly enhance lane-related structures and may be sensitive to reflections and illumination changes.

Additionally, a color transformation inspired by lane detection approaches in the autonomous driving domain was explored. Specifically, the R+G-Btransformation (commonly referred to as rgminusb) was considered:
I=R+G-B
This transformation increases the contrast between bright lane markings and darker surfaces by emphasizing red and green channel intensities while suppressing the blue channel. In the context of bowling lanes, where wooden surfaces and lane markings often present distinct chromatic responses, this representation proved particularly effective at highlighting relevant boundaries.

To further improve robustness against illumination changes, Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was also investigated as an alternative transformation strategy. In this approach, the RGB channels are projected into a lower-dimensional representation, retaining only the first principal component, which captures the largest variance in the image. Since structural lane features often dominate the variance distribution, this transformation can enhance boundary visibility under challenging lighting conditions.
Finally, transformations based on the HSV color space were considered, motivated by the observation that lane boundaries may exhibit stronger separability in chromatic information than in raw intensity values. In particular, the hue component was evaluated as a potential representation for emphasizing boundary transitions.
After empirical evaluation across multiple bowling videos, the R+G-B transformation yielded the most consistent performance for the detection of visible lane boundaries and was therefore selected as the default preprocessing step in the proposed pipeline.


Image smoothing
Before edge detection, Gaussian blurring is applied to reduce high-frequency noise and suppress small intensity variations that may generate false edge responses. In bowling lane imagery, reflections on the lane surface and texture variations can introduce spurious gradients that negatively affect boundary detection.
A Gaussian filter with kernel size 5\times5 is applied to the single-channel transformed image. This preprocessing step improves the stability of subsequent edge detection methods while preserving the dominant structural information of the lane boundaries.



Edge detection
After image smoothing, edge detection is applied to extract candidate structures corresponding to lane boundaries. Three classical gradient-based and intensity-based methods were evaluated: Sobel, Canny, and Laplacian filters. These methods differ in how they estimate intensity discontinuities and in their sensitivity to noise and texture variations.
The Sobel operator computes first-order image gradients and is used to approximate horizontal and vertical intensity changes. It is particularly useful for emphasizing directional structures, making it suitable for detecting lane boundaries with a strong geometric orientation.
The Canny detector follows a multi-stage process involving gradient computation, non-maximum suppression, and hysteresis thresholding. This approach typically produces thinner and more connected edges, but its performance is sensitive to threshold selection and illumination variability.
The Laplacian operator is a second-order derivative method that detects regions of rapid intensity change regardless of direction. While it can highlight fine structures, it is also more sensitive to noise and may produce fragmented edge maps in low-contrast regions.
All three methods were implemented and evaluated across multiple bowling videos. Based on qualitative performance and stability of the resulting edge maps, the Sobel operator was selected as the primary edge detector for the proposed pipeline. Depending on the boundary being estimated, the gradient direction is adapted to emphasize either horizontal or vertical structures, improving the extraction of relevant lane edges prior to line fitting.

Line extraction using the Hough transform
Following edge detection, line candidates corresponding to lane boundaries are extracted using the probabilistic Hough transform. This method is particularly suitable for lane detection tasks, as it enables robust identification of parametric line segments even in the presence of incomplete or fragmented edges.
The probabilistic Hough transform maps edge points in the image space to a parameter space and identifies line segments based on an accumulator voting scheme. In this work, the HoughLinesP implementation is used, which directly returns finite line segments defined by their endpoints. This formulation is computationally efficient and better suited to real-time processing compared to the standard Hough transform.
For each frame, the detected line segments are filtered based on geometric constraints consistent with the expected lane structure. Specifically, lines are rejected if they do not satisfy minimum length requirements or if their orientation deviates significantly from the expected direction of the corresponding lane boundary. This filtering step reduces spurious detections arising from texture patterns, reflections, or irrelevant scene structures.
The remaining line segments are then ranked or selected depending on the specific boundary being estimated. Since multiple valid line hypotheses may exist, especially in textured regions of the lane, additional geometric heuristics are introduced in later stages of the pipeline to disambiguate the final boundary selection.
The Hough transform thus serves as a mid-level representation that converts pixel-level edge information into structured geometric primitives, which are subsequently refined using boundary-specific selection strategies.

Bottom border detection
The first step is to convert to grayscale and perform gaussian blurring on the image. Then,  the frame is cropped so only a specific bottom portion of the image is visible. After analyzing multiple bowling videos, we determined this should be done in the following crop region: [0.6, 1.0, 0.15, 0.85].
Next, the edges are to be detected. After this, the houghlinesp method is used to rank the best lines. Finally, a final filter is applied, to filter the lines that don’t have an inclination that aligns with the expected one.

After testing the different combinations, the best results were obtained using the r+g-b conversion equation, and sobel filter for bottom lane boundary so this is the chosen one for the detection pipeline

Lateral borders detection
The first step is to perform the standardized preprocessing mentioned before (grayscale conversion + gaussian blur).
Later, the edges are to be detected. In this case, the frame is not cropped, and the sobel filter is used but with a different direction (it detects vertical lines). After this, the houghlinesp method is used to rank the best lines, and lines are filtered out if they have a slope which is too horizontal.
However, a different approach is taken in this case. One additional element is needed. With the bottom lane, there is only one line which is common to all the lanes. This is not the case for the lateral boundaries, where there are multiple parallel lines (one pair for each of the lanes in the bowling alley). For this reason, (and because we cant a priori know which lane is to be tracked), we assume that we know the center point (or any point) inside the lane that we want to detect. For simplicity, we use the center point of the image (as normally the central lane is the one we want to detect), but this point could be changed if we wanted to detect a lane in a different position. We use this point in a heuristic to detect the lateral boundaries. 

We draw a horizontal line that passes through this point. Then we intersect all the detected lines with this horizontal line. We calculate the distance from the intersection to the center point.
Then, the heuristic used to select the 2 lines is that the right boundary is the closest detected line to the right of the lane center point, and the left boundary is the closest detected line to the left of the lane center point.


Top border detection
Multiple methods were explored to try to detect this lane. Initially, a similar approach was explored, but with no success due to the nature of this line. It is blocked by the pins. The second approach was to try to use sift, to detect the pins in a scale invariant way. However, due to the lack of keypoints in a pin, and because of the small size of them in the videos (pins are far away from camera), the final method chosen was to use template matching exploiting the knowledge that we sort of know the size the pins will have.
For additional support, no lateral cropping is performed, to leverage pins in other lanes, in order to estimate the boundary more robustly and have more points to support the calculation of the line. 
For the template matching to be multi scale (pins in videos have slightly different sizes, and we must take this into account), multiple sizes are tested (from 80% of the template pin size to 200% of the pin size). In this way, we can make the detection more robust for different videos with different apparent pin sizes.

After performing template matching along a frame, non maximum suppression is used to filter out slightly different detections of the same pin. The midpoints of the bounding boxes are stored. (Actually, storing the bottom of the bounding box, was a better estimation of the real height at which the top lane border is).
Also, after obtaining the midpoints, ransac Is used for outlier removal This helps filterin out false positive detections elsewhere in the image.
After removing outliers, a first degree polynomial is fit with least squares. This is the resulting top lane boundary.
Lane rectification and 3D Reconstruction
A bowling lane has known dimensions, which are 60 feet long and 42 inches wide [lane dimensions] (for us bowling lanes, the ones used in the videos – European ones are slightly longer). The output of the lane detection are the coordinates of the 4 lane edges in the image plane.
Thus, by knowing the dimensions of the lane and the coordinates of its edges in the image plane, we can compute the image to plane homography.

As we also know that the ten-pin bowling ball has a diameter of 21.83 cm[ball dimensions], and as the ball movement is constrained to the floor (the ball is always in contact with the floor), we can estimate the 3d coordinates of the ball with respect to a chosen point of the lane by using the planar trajectory of the ball on the lane, and adding the radius of the ball. This would return the center of mass in 3d with respect to the lane.
